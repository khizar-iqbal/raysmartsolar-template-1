Here is the whole purchase in one paragraph, as of July 2026: a good 5 kW system costs about **Rs 5.5–9 lakh** installed, cuts a typical Rs 25,000 bill by well over half (more with a battery), and pays for itself in **3–5 years** — *provided* you buy the right size, with real hardware, from a company that will still exist in year three. Those three provisos are where people get burned. This guide is every check we would run if we were buying for our own mother’s house: the economics, the sizing maths, the hardware tiers, the ten scams and how to catch each one, and the twelve questions that sort honest installers from the rest. Take it to three companies, including us.

## 1. Is solar right for you? The honest checklist

Solar is right for you when most of these are true:

- **You own the roof** — or have the owner’s written consent.
- **Your bill regularly clears Rs 15–20,000 a month.** Below that the maths still works, but payback stretches; at Rs 8,000 a month it can stretch past most people’s patience. We’d rather tell you that now.
- **You have ~55 sq ft of shade-free roof per kW** — after subtracting the water tank, the mumty and the neighbour’s shadow.
- **Someone uses power in daylight** — or you’re open to a battery to shift the sun into your evening.
- **You’ll stay put 3+ years**, long enough to collect the payback you paid for.
- **Your sanctioned load can cover the system size** — a 2026 rule; it’s printed on your bill and we explain it in the glossary.

When to wait: you’re moving or rebuilding the roof soon, your bill is small, or your usage is about to change drastically (big renovation, shifting a business home). Solar rewards a settled situation.

## 2. The honest 2026 economics

Two numbers now run every solar decision in Pakistan. A unit you *import* from the grid costs most urban households **Rs 40–65 all-in** at typical usage, as of mid-2026, and the direction of travel is up — subsidy reform is scheduled to push effective rates higher for many. A unit you *export* earns a floating regulated rate — roughly **Rs 8–13** under the net-billing rules that replaced net metering for new connections in February 2026.

So the money is in the units you *don’t buy*, not the ones you sell. That has three consequences:

1. **Size to your usage, not your roof.** Oversized arrays now sell their surplus cheap. The right-sized system routes generation into your own load.
2. **Batteries earn a place.** Storing the afternoon and spending it in the evening turns Rs 8–13 exports into Rs 40–65 avoided imports.
3. **Payback is 3–5 years, not 2.** The one-to-one era produced faster paybacks; anyone still promising them is quoting a dead policy. A well-designed 2026 system still cuts most bills by 70–90% — the grid’s own prices guarantee the arithmetic.

The [calculator](/calculator) runs your bill through exactly this model with every assumption printed. If a company’s savings claims can’t survive being written down with assumptions attached, they weren’t savings — they were sales.

## 3. Sizing from your bill, in sixty seconds

The chain: **rupees → units → kilowatts → panels → roof.**

1. Read “Units Consumed” off your bill (or estimate: Rs 25,000 ≈ ~540 units at mid-2026 Lahore tariffs).
2. Divide by your city’s yield — Lahore averages ~118 units per kW per month across the year.
3. Divide the kilowatts by ~615 W (today’s typical Tier-1 panel) for the panel count.
4. Multiply kilowatts by ~55 sq ft for the roof space.

| Monthly bill | ≈ Units | Sensible size | Panels | Roof space |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Rs 25,000 | ~540 | 5 kW | 9 | ~275 sq ft (≈1.2 marla) |
| Rs 45,000 | ~765 | 7 kW | 12 | ~385 sq ft (≈1.7 marla) |
| Rs 80,000 | ~1,330 | 10–15 kW | 17–25 | ~550–825 sq ft (≈2.5–3.7 marla) |

One winter warning: Lahore’s December output is roughly half of June’s — short days plus smog season — so honest sizing uses annual averages. Any projection built on summer numbers is fiction with a spreadsheet. The [full sizing walkthrough](/learn/how-many-solar-panels) shows the working.

## 4. Hardware: what the tiers actually mean

### “Tier-1” is a bankability list, not a quality medal

The Tier-1 label comes from a finance-industry list of panel makers whose projects banks reliably fund. It signals a large, solvent manufacturer — which matters enormously for a 25-year warranty — but it is not a lab test result. In Pakistan’s market it still does one useful job: it separates traceable brands (Jinko, LONGi, JA Solar, Canadian Solar) from mystery modules. The sharper question is the next one.

### Documented vs undocumented — the question that matters more

**Documented** panels entered Pakistan through official import channels: customs paperwork exists, serial numbers verify in the manufacturer’s own app, and the local office will honour the warranty. **Undocumented** panels came through the grey channel — sometimes genuine, sometimes relabelled B-grade — and either way the warranty is a rumour. Wholesale Tier-1 rates were about **Rs 40–48 per watt** in early July 2026; if a quote implies panels far cheaper than that, they are not the panels being claimed. The check is simple and takes two minutes: scan the serial in the official brand app *before final payment*, with the installer watching.

### Inverters

The inverter is the component most likely to need service within a decade, so buy a name with a real local service network — Huawei, Sungrow, GoodWe, Solis and Growatt are the common serious choices here, with warranties typically 5–10 years. And one Pakistan-specific check that outranks brand taste: for net metering, the exact model must be on your DISCO’s approved list. A non-approved inverter can mean exported units that are never credited.

### Batteries

Lithium (LiFePO4) is the 2026 default: 80–90% usable capacity, thousands of cycles, no maintenance. Reputable brands — Pylontech, Dyness, GoodWe, Knox, BYD — ran roughly **Rs 40,000–55,000 per kWh** as of mid-2026; a hybrid setup with one ~5 kWh battery adds about Rs 3–5 lakh over on-grid. Tubular lead-acid is cheaper upfront but delivers far less over its life. Whatever is quoted, demand the chemistry, brand, kWh and cycle warranty in writing.

### The invisible 15%: structure and wiring

Steel, cable and earthing are where cheap quotes cut first, because you can’t see any of it from the ground. Insist on: hot-dip galvanised or anodised-aluminium structure, named DC cable brand and size, proper breakers and surge protection, and a real earthing arrangement. Skimping here is how systems underperform in year one and catch fire in year three.

## 5. The scam checklist — ten patterns, and how to catch each one

These are the ten recurring complaint patterns in Pakistan’s solar market, per consumer reporting through 2025–26. None of them survives a prepared buyer.

**1. The vanishing company.** A social-media-only outfit with glossy posts takes a fat advance and dissolves. *Catch it:* visit the physical office — no office, no deal. Check the company against PPIB’s published list of certified installers (PPIB absorbed AEDB in 2023; the list is on ppib.gov.pk). Pay in stages against delivered work, never lump-sum.

**2. “Free solar panels” / the government-subsidy pitch.** Someone claims they can get you free or subsidised panels through a scheme. *Catch it:* ask for the official programme name and look it up on the government’s own site — the pitch almost never survives that. As of July 2026 there is no general free-panels programme for ordinary households; a company that opens with a fiction will not improve later.

**3. Bait-and-switch pricing.** Rs 4.5 lakh on the phone becomes Rs 6.5 lakh after the visit — wiring extra, structure extra, “net-metering charges” extra. *Catch it:* only compare final written post-survey quotes, line by line. Then ask, in writing: “Is there any scenario in which I pay more than this figure?”

**4. B-grade or relabelled panels sold as Tier-1.** Lower real wattage, no valid warranty, sometimes a famous name freshly stickered on. *Catch it:* serial-verify every panel in the manufacturer’s app before final payment; ask to see import documentation. A per-watt price far below the wholesale band (Rs 40–48/W, July 2026) is the tell.

**5. The wrong inverter for net metering.** A model your DISCO hasn’t approved — or one that can’t export — means your surplus units are never credited. *Catch it:* get the exact model number in the quote and check it against the DISCO’s approved list before signing. Any installer who can’t show you that list hasn’t filed many applications.

**6. The net-metering delay trap.** The system is installed, the file “is in process” — for a year. Meanwhile your exports go to the grid unpaid. *Catch it:* put the filing date, the documented follow-up schedule and the honest current DISCO timeline in the contract, and ask what happens if the delay is the installer’s fault. Vague answers now become vague answers later.

**7. Cheap cables, breakers and earthing.** Invisible on day one; losses, trips and fire risk by year one. *Catch it:* the quote must *name* the DC cable brand and size, breaker types, surge protection and the earthing spec. “Standard wiring included” is not a specification.

**8. Overselling the size.** A 15 kW pitch for a 10 kW life. Under net billing the punishment is monthly: the surplus exports at Rs 8–13 while you still buy evening units at Rs 40+. *Catch it:* run the sizing chain from section 3 yourself, ask what daytime self-use share the design assumes, and remember the 2026 rule — the system can’t exceed your sanctioned load anyway.

**9. Fake warranties.** <span lang="ur-Latn">“25 saal ki guarantee, sir”</span> — spoken by a company that is 25 months old. *Catch it:* warranties must be manufacturer-backed, written, and traceable to your serial numbers. Ask exactly who honours a panel claim in year 14 and where their office is. The installer’s own workmanship warranty should be in writing too.

**10. The monitoring-app hostage.** The installer sets up the monitoring account under their own credentials and keeps admin rights; you can’t see faults, and your generation data lives with them. *Catch it:* before the crew leaves, the owner account goes on **your** email and number, with the installer downgraded to guest access. Watch it happen on your own phone.

## 6. Twelve questions to ask any installer — including us

Print these. Good answers are specific, numeric and boring. Bad answers are adjectives.

1. **“Which exact panel model and wattage, and are they documented?”** Good: names the model, offers serial verification and the datasheet without flinching.
2. **“Which inverter model — and show me it’s on the DISCO’s approved list.”** Good: a model number and the list itself, not <span lang="ur-Latn">“sir, sab approved hai”</span>.
3. **“What’s my sanctioned load, and does this system fit within it?”** Good: they asked for your bill before answering — because it’s printed there.
4. **“Walk me through how you sized this.”** Good: your units, your city’s yield, your daytime usage — the chain from section 3 with your numbers in it.
5. **“What will it generate in December versus June?”** Good: December lands around half of June, smog gets mentioned, and the payback maths already used the annual figure.
6. **“What exactly is included — structure, cabling, earthing, fees, taxes?”** Good: an itemised quote where every one of those is a line, and taxes are shown separately.
7. **“Who files the net-metering application, and what’s the honest current timeline?”** Good: a real range for your DISCO as of this month, plus who chases the file and how often.
8. **“What does each warranty cover, and who honours it locally?”** Good: manufacturer-backed terms, a local office you could actually visit, everything in writing.
9. **“What happens during a power cut?”** Good, for on-grid: “it switches off — that’s a safety requirement” followed by an honest battery conversation. Anyone who says a plain on-grid system runs through outages has just failed the exam.
10. **“What’s the payment schedule?”** Good: staged against milestones — modest booking, majority against delivery and installation, balance at commissioning. Bad: most of the money before a single panel arrives.
11. **“Who owns the monitoring account?”** Good: “You do. We take guest access for support.”
12. **“What maintenance will this need, and what do you charge for it?”** Good: a cleaning cadence (more in smog season), inverter checks, what the first year includes, and real prices for afterwards.

## 7. Financing reality

Cash remains the cheapest way to buy — but as of mid-2026 you have real alternatives, all market-rate: bank solar loans run at KIBOR-linked markups of roughly **15–22% a year** (Meezan for Islamic financing, Bank Alfalah’s green line from 4 kW up, JS Bank with longer tenors for businesses), installers and fintechs offer 6–24-month instalment plans on smaller tickets, and businesses above 50 kW can look at leases, ijarah, or zero-capex PPA structures. Two honest notes. First, the old SBP concessional solar scheme ended in 2023 — treat anyone advertising it as out of date. Second, financing stretches payback because the lender keeps part of the saving; always ask for the with-financing cashflow before deciding. Rates move with KIBOR — get live quotes, not brochure numbers.

## 8. Net billing in 200 words

Since 9 February 2026, new solar connections operate under NEPRA’s Prosumer Regulations — **net billing**. You buy grid units at your normal retail tariff, taxes included. You sell surplus units at the National Average Energy Purchase Price (NAEPP), a floating rate NEPRA revises — reported around Rs 8–13 per unit as of mid-2026. Settlement is monthly in rupees; credits roll forward, with quarterly payouts. Agreements run five years. Your system cannot exceed your sanctioned load, tops out at 1 MW, and new connections pause on transformers that are already 80% solar-loaded. Costs, as of July 2026: a Rs 1,000-per-kW NEPRA processing fee plus the bidirectional meter (reported Rs 18,500–70,000 depending on DISCO and meter type). Approval realistically takes 4–14 weeks depending on the DISCO. If you connected *before* the change, your old one-to-one terms hold until your agreement expires — but expanding your system voids that protection, so take advice before adding a single panel. The design lesson for new buyers is one line: **consume your own sunshine first; export only the leftovers.** The [full policy story](/learn/net-metering-2026) has the history and the fine print.

## 9. Maintenance: what owning it is actually like

Less than a car, more than nothing:

- **Cleaning.** Dust is the silent thief. A rinse/wipe every couple of weeks in dry months; extra attention from November to February, when smog plus dirt can cost 30% or more of winter output. Ground-accessible spray setups make this a ten-minute job.
- **Watching the app.** A weekly glance at generation catches most faults early. (This is why *you* must own the monitoring account.)
- **Annually.** An electrical inspection: connections, breakers, earthing, structure bolts. Cheap insurance against expensive surprises.
- **Batteries.** Lithium is close to maintenance-free — keep it ventilated and watch its cycles in the app. Tubular needs distilled-water top-ups and ventilation, on a schedule.
- **Lifespans.** Tier-1 panels carry ~25-year performance and ~12-year product warranties; inverters carry 5–10 years and are the component most likely to need attention around year eight — worth budgeting for mentally.

If output sags beyond the season’s normal dip, call your installer. A company that answers that call in year four is the company you were actually shopping for in year zero.
