If you remember one thing, make it this: **an on-grid solar system switches itself off during load-shedding.** That single fact sorts the three architectures. On-grid is the cheapest per kW and right for feeders that rarely trip. Hybrid — solar plus battery, still connected to the grid — is the 2026 sweet spot for most Pakistani homes. Off-grid is for the farm and the tube well, not for Gulberg.

## The three, side by side

| | On-grid | Hybrid | Off-grid |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Grid connection | Yes | Yes | No |
| During an outage | Shuts off | Essentials keep running | Runs — it’s all you have |
| Battery | None | Yes, sized to essentials | Yes, large banks |
| Cost position | Cheapest per kW | On-grid + ~Rs 3–5 lakh per 5 kWh | Highest by far |
| Best for | Stable feeders, daytime-heavy use | Most homes in 2026 | Remote sites, agri loads |

## On-grid: cheapest, with one honest catch

An on-grid system is panels, an inverter, and the grid as your buffer: solar feeds the house by day, surplus exports, and the grid covers nights. No battery to buy or replace, so it delivers the lowest cost per kW and the fastest payback *if* your power seldom goes out.

The catch is called **anti-islanding**, and no honest installer will hide it: when the grid fails, a grid-tied inverter must disconnect within a fraction of a second. This is not stinginess — it is a worldwide safety requirement that stops your roof back-feeding a line some LESCO lineman believes is dead. The result is the great Pakistani irony: the outage that made you want solar is precisely when a plain on-grid system gives you nothing, even at noon.

On-grid still wins when: your feeder is genuinely stable, your usage is daytime-heavy, the budget is tight, or you’re a business with a generator already handling outages.

## Hybrid: the 2026 sweet spot

A hybrid system adds a battery and a hybrid inverter, and it earns its premium twice over:

1. **Backup.** When the grid drops, the essentials — fans, lights, fridge, Wi-Fi — carry on without a flicker. No generator noise, no petrol run at midnight.
2. **Better economics under net billing.** Since February 2026, exports earn a floating Rs 8–13 per unit while imported units cost Rs 40–65 all-in. A battery stores your 2pm surplus and spends it at 8pm — turning cheap exports into expensive avoided imports. Typical homes jump from using roughly a third of their solar directly to around 70%.

The price of admission, as of mid-2026: roughly **Rs 3–5 lakh per ~5 kWh** of lithium storage over the equivalent on-grid system, hybrid inverter premium included. Not small money — but it is the difference between owning backup power and owning a discount on sunny afternoons. ## Off-grid: for the farmhouse, not the city

Off-grid means no grid at all — your array and battery bank must carry the worst week of a smoggy December, not the average day in June. That forces you to oversize everything, which is why cost per usable unit balloons. Inside a city, the grid — whatever its moods — is a very cheap backup to keep.

Where off-grid is rational: land the grid never reached, agricultural tube wells, cattle sheds and remote sites where a new connection costs more than the system. There, it isn’t a lifestyle choice; it’s the only sensible engineering.

## How bad is load-shedding, honestly?

We sell batteries, so believe us when we understate the problem. The 2026 picture, as reported:

- The April 2026 national plan scheduled about **2.25 hours a day** of urban cuts, placed in the 5pm–1am window; actual urban outages ran under two hours as of mid-April. K-Electric sat outside the national plan but runs its own loss-based cuts.
- The structural story is feeder-level: high-loss feeders (over 40% losses/theft) face **4–6 hours or more** daily, and the worst rural pockets run far past that. Your address’s feeder decides your reality.
- Summer (May–August) stresses the grid hardest — and beyond the schedule sit the unscheduled trips, maintenance shutdowns and voltage sags that never make the news but do reset your router.

So no, urban Lahore does not face “8 hours of daily load-shedding”, whatever a salesman needs you to fear. The honest case for backup is unpredictability and evenings — which is a real case, priced honestly.

## Backup sizing: the two-minute maths

A Pakistani home’s essentials — three or four fans, LED lights, the fridge, router and phone chargers — draw roughly **0.4–0.5 kWh per hour**. From there:

- A **5 kWh lithium battery** ≈ **8–10 hours of essentials**, after usable-depth and conversion losses.
- A typical 2–3 hour evening cut uses only 1–1.5 kWh — the same battery then still carries the night load with room to spare.
- Add a 1.5-ton inverter AC and the picture changes fast: that one machine draws roughly 1–1.8 kWh per hour on its own, tripling the drain. Whole-house backup is possible; it is simply a much bigger battery bill.

The rule: list what genuinely must run in an outage, multiply by the hours you actually face, add ~20% headroom. We size backup to that list — not to fear.

## Lithium vs tubular: the chemistry question

**LiFePO4 (lithium)** is what the numbers favour in 2026: 80–90% of capacity usable, thousands of cycles, roughly a decade of design life, no fumes, no topping up. Reputable brands — Pylontech, Dyness, GoodWe, Knox, BYD — ran about Rs 40,000–55,000 per kWh as of mid-2026.

**Tubular lead-acid** is the familiar UPS-era choice: noticeably cheaper upfront, but only ~50% of capacity safely usable, far fewer cycles (daily cycling wears one out in a few years), plus watering and ventilation to manage. Per unit of electricity actually delivered over its life, lithium usually wins comfortably. Tubular still has a place where the budget is hard-capped and outages are occasional rather than daily.

## Choosing in one minute

- Feeder rarely trips, budget tight, daytime-heavy usage → **on-grid**.
- Outages hurt, evenings are when you live, work-from-home or kids studying → **hybrid**, battery sized to your essentials list.
- No grid at the site → **off-grid**, and a proper engineering conversation.
