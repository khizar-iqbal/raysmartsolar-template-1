For a typical Lahore home the chain looks like this: **a Rs 25,000 bill ≈ ~540 units ≈ 5 kW ≈ nine modern panels ≈ 275 sq ft of clear roof** — a bit over one marla. That chain is the entire method. Here it is step by step, so you can run it on your own bill and sanity-check anyone who quotes you.

## Step 1: bill → units

The best source is the bill itself — “Units Consumed” is printed on it. Average a few months if you can (summer months lie upward, winter months lie downward).

If you only remember the rupees, you can estimate. Because tariffs run in slabs, each extra unit costs more than the last, so the relationship isn’t a straight line. As of mid-2026, for an unprotected Lahore household, all-in bills map roughly like this:

| Monthly bill (all-in) | ≈ Units consumed |
| --- | --- |
| Rs 11,000 | ~300 |
| Rs 22,000 | ~500 |
| Rs 25,000 | ~540 |
| Rs 48,000 | ~800 |
| Rs 72,000 | ~1,200 |

These are estimates built from mid-2026 tariff schedules including taxes and adjustments — tariffs get revised every year and adjusted monthly, so treat the table as a guide, not gospel. The [calculator](/calculator) uses the live model, and at the survey we work from your actual bills.

## Step 2: units → kilowatts

In Lahore, one kilowatt of well-installed solar generates about **118 units per month averaged across the year** (from roughly 1,411 kWh per kW per year — Global Solar Atlas data). So:

**540 units ÷ 118 ≈ 4.6 kW → a 5 kW system.**

City matters: Karachi averages ~133 units per kW per month, Islamabad ~125. And the annual average hides the seasons — a Lahore June can produce roughly double a Lahore December, because winter brings short days *and* smog season (late October to January), which can cut output 30–50% on the worst days. Honest sizing uses the year-round number, never the June number.

## Step 3: kilowatts → panels

Modern Tier-1 residential panels are big — 585–645 W each as of 2026, call it **~615 W**. So:

**5,000 W ÷ 615 W ≈ 9 panels.**

If your mental maths still runs on the 250 W panels of a few years ago, you’ll overcount panels by more than double. Nine panels is a modest, tidy array — which is why 5 kW fits on most Lahore homes without drama.

## Step 4: panels → roof space

Rule of thumb: about **55 sq ft per kW**, including walkways and row spacing. A 5 kW system needs ~275 sq ft — about **1.2 marla** at Punjab’s 225 sq ft marla. Before you celebrate, subtract the roof you can’t use: the water tank, the mumty, shade lines from parapets and taller neighbours. Shade on even one panel drags down its whole string, so the *usable* number matters more than the total.

## The same maths at three bill sizes

| Monthly bill | ≈ Units | Raw need | Sensible size | Panels (~615 W) | Roof space |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Rs 25,000 | ~540 | ~4.6 kW | 5 kW | 9 | ~275 sq ft (≈1.2 marla) |
| Rs 45,000 | ~765 | ~6.5 kW | 7 kW | 12 | ~385 sq ft (≈1.7 marla) |
| Rs 80,000 | ~1,330 | ~11 kW | 10–15 kW* | 17–25 | ~550–825 sq ft (≈2.5–3.7 marla) |

*Lahore yields, mid-2026 tariff estimates. Above ~10 kW the honest answer depends on your daytime/evening split — which is the next section.*

## Why bigger stopped being better

Until February 2026, oversizing was free money: every exported unit cancelled an imported one. Under the new **net billing** rules, exports earn a floating Rs 8–13 per unit while imports cost you Rs 40–65 all-in — so surplus generation is worth roughly a fifth of what self-consumed generation is worth. Oversizing now means buying extra hardware to sell its output cheap.

What matters instead is your **self-use share** — the slice of solar output your household consumes directly. A typical home with nobody in at midday uses only ~35% of generation directly; the rest exports at the low rate. Add a battery and the afternoon surplus gets stored and spent in the evening, lifting self-use to ~70%.

That is why the Rs 80,000 house in the table gets a range, not a number. If there’s daytime load (AC running, someone home, a shop below) or a battery in the plan, 15 kW can earn its place. If the house is empty until 6pm and there’s no battery, a 10 kW system usually beats a 15 kW one on actual money returned. The [full comparison of system types](/learn/on-grid-hybrid-off-grid) covers the battery side of this.

## Sanity-check any quote with this chain

The chain runs both ways. If someone quotes 22 panels for your Rs 25,000 bill, the maths catches them: ~540 units needs ~4.6 kW, which is nine big panels, not twenty-two. If someone promises a 3 kW system will erase a Rs 45,000 bill, the maths catches that too — 3 kW makes ~350 units a month against your ~765.
