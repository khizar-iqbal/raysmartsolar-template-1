Net metering — the unit-for-unit exchange — closed to new applicants on **9 February 2026**. New connections now run on **net billing**: you pay full retail for every unit you import (Rs 40–65 all-in for most households, as of mid-2026) and are paid a floating regulated rate — reported around **Rs 8–13 per unit** — for what you export. If you already have net metering, your existing terms survive until your agreement expires. Solar still pays; it just pays differently now, and systems have to be designed for it.

## Thirty seconds of vocabulary

- **Net metering (the 2015 rules):** every exported unit cancelled an imported one, one-to-one. Leftover surplus was bought at ~Rs 25–27 per unit late in the era.
- **Net billing (the 2026 rules):** imports and exports are priced separately — imports at your full retail tariff, exports at a much lower wholesale-linked rate. Settlement is monthly, in rupees.
- **NAEPP:** the National Average Energy Purchase Price — the rate NEPRA sets and revises from time to time. It is what new prosumers earn per exported unit.

## How we got here

### 2015–2024: the one-to-one years

NEPRA’s 2015 regulations built the rooftop boom: seven-year agreements, systems allowed up to 1.5x your sanctioned load, and a meter that spun both ways at equal value. Combined with brutal grid tariffs and crashing panel prices, it worked spectacularly — a reported 283,000+ net-metered consumers by December 2024, with capacity passing 4 GW by March 2025 and 5 GW by mid-2025, per pv-magazine. In the background, Pakistan imported some 17 GW of panels in 2024 alone (per Ember, the world’s third-largest importer that year). The government increasingly argued that paying retail rates for daytime exports shifted costs onto everyone without panels.

### March 2025: the Rs 10 scare that never happened

On 13 March 2025, the ECC approved cutting the buyback rate from ~Rs 27 to **Rs 10 per unit**. The backlash was loud and immediate — and by late March the federal cabinet had declined to ratify it, sending the policy back for review. The Rs 10 rate never took effect. But it previewed the direction of travel, and the industry spent the rest of 2025 waiting for the other shoe.

### February 2026: net billing arrives for real

The shoe dropped on 9 February 2026, when NEPRA notified the **NEPRA (Prosumer) Regulations, 2026**, replacing the 2015 regime for new connections. The essentials, as reported:

- Exports are paid at the **NAEPP** — computed around Rs 8.13 at notification by some outlets, reported as Rs 11–13 by others. Treat it as **roughly Rs 8–13, floating, revised by NEPRA**.
- Imports are billed at your **full retail tariff**, taxes and all.
- Settlement is **monthly in rupees**; net credits roll forward, with payouts quarterly.
- Agreements now run **5 years** (previously 7).
- Your system may not exceed your **sanctioned load** (previously 1.5x), and is capped at 1 MW.
- New connections pause on any transformer where solar already fills **80% of its rated capacity**.
- Systems of **250 kW and above** need technical studies before approval.

### The pushback, and the grandfathering amendment

Within days, existing consumers were reading headlines that suggested their deal was dead too. The Prime Minister intervened publicly; NEPRA floated a draft amendment on 16 February 2026 and finalised it in early April, with retrospective effect from 9 February. The result: **existing net-metering consumers keep their original terms — the one-to-one exchange and the old buyback — until their current agreement expires.**

One serious caveat rode along: any *material modification* to your system — adding panels, upgrading to a bigger inverter — forfeits the protection and moves you to net billing. And applications **submitted before 8 February 2026** were ordered processed under the old rules.

### May 2026: clearing the stuck files

Pre-deadline applications piled up at the DISCOs. On 24 May 2026, the Power Minister ordered them to clear a backlog of 1,355 stalled pre-8-February applications by 1 June. Files do move when someone pushes — which is exactly why we chase ours weekly.

## What connecting costs, as of July 2026

- **NEPRA processing fee:** Rs 1,000 per kW, non-refundable, now applying to all system sizes (introduced in April 2026 coverage of the regulations).
- **Bidirectional (“green”) meter:** reported anywhere from Rs 18,500 to Rs 70,000 depending on DISCO and meter type — quote it as a range until your DISCO confirms.
- **Interconnection costs** sit with the prosumer under the 2026 regulations.

The paper clock says: application acknowledged in 5 working days, technical review in 15 days, meter installed 15 days after payment. Reality varies with each DISCO’s workload — recent LESCO cases ran 10–15 weeks amid its backlog, while K-Electric often completes residential cases in 4–6 weeks. ## What it means for you

### Buying new in 2026

Three design consequences, all of them fixable at the drawing board:

1. **Self-use first.** A unit you consume yourself is worth the Rs 40–65 you didn’t pay the grid; a unit you export earns roughly Rs 8–13. Same panel, same sunshine, about a five-fold difference. Good design routes generation into your own daytime load.
2. **Batteries earn their keep.** Storing the afternoon surplus and spending it in the evening beats selling cheap at 2pm and buying dear at 8pm.
3. **Right-size, don’t max-size.** The old logic — fill the roof, the meter will pay for it — is dead. Size to your usage, not your roof area. Our [sizing guide](/learn/how-many-solar-panels) walks the maths.

Payback on a well-designed system still lands around **3–5 years**, because grid power is expensive enough to carry the arithmetic on its own. The [calculator](/calculator) runs your bill under the 2026 rules, assumptions printed.

### Already on net metering

You keep your terms until your agreement expires — after which renewal happens under whatever rules prevail then (net billing, as things stand). Two practical notes. First, **do not expand casually**: adding panels or a bigger inverter can void your grandfathered terms. Occasionally the expansion is still worth it; often it isn’t; get the maths done before touching anything. Second, keep your paperwork — agreement, licence dates, meter records — somewhere you can find it.

### Applied before 8 February 2026

Your file is entitled to processing under the **old rules**, by ministerial direction, and the May 2026 order told DISCOs to clear the backlog. ## The bottom line

Net billing didn’t kill solar. It killed *lazy* solar — the oversized, export-everything arrays that made sense only under one-to-one exchange. Sized to your real usage, with evenings carried by a battery where they need to be, a 2026 system still cuts most bills by well over half and pays for itself in a few years.
