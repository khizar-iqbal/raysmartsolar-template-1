Commercial grid power costs roughly **Rs 45–60 per unit all-in** as of mid-2026, and most businesses burn most of their power in daylight — exactly when panels produce. That pairing is why well-designed commercial solar in Pakistan often pays back in **2–3.5 years**, faster than almost any machine on your books. Here is the maths with its assumptions showing, plus the caps, the Sunday problem, and the financing routes from bank markup to zero-capex.

## Why the maths favours businesses

- **The tariff.** Commercial connections pay about Rs 45–60 per unit once taxes and adjustments land (as of mid-2026) — among the most expensive routine electricity anywhere in your cost sheet. Every unit your roof replaces saves the full amount.
- **The timing.** Shops, offices, schools and factories run roughly 8am–6pm — solar hours. A daytime operation typically self-consumes **60% or more** of generation with no battery at all, versus ~35% for a home. Under the 2026 net-billing rules, that coincidence is the whole game.
- **The scale.** Cost per kW falls as systems grow: at the 50–100 kW range, turnkey pricing ran roughly **Rs 88,000–110,000 per kW** as of July 2026, against Rs 110,000+ per kW at household sizes.

## A worked example: 50 kW on a factory roof

All figures are mid-2026 estimates; your proposal gets real ones from your own bills.

- **Cost:** 50 kW × Rs 88–110k ≈ **Rs 44–55 lakh** turnkey.
- **Generation:** central Punjab yields ~1,400+ units per kW per year → **~70,000 units a year**.
- **Self-consumed (60%):** 42,000 units × Rs 45–60 → **Rs 19–25 lakh a year** avoided.
- **Exported (40%):** 28,000 units × Rs 8–13 (the floating NAEPP rate) → **Rs 2.2–3.6 lakh a year**.
- **Total benefit:** ≈ **Rs 21–29 lakh a year** → simple payback ≈ **1.5–2.6 years on paper**.

Real life then adds cleaning, inverter servicing, panel degradation, the odd downtime week, seasonal dips and financing costs — which is why we quote **2–3.5 years** as the honest planning band rather than the brochure number. After payback, the system keeps producing for two decades at a marginal cost near zero, which quietly converts your most volatile overhead into a fixed one.

## The Sunday problem, and other honesty

On days you’re closed, generation doesn’t stop — it exports at Rs 8–13, not Rs 45–60. For a six-day operation that’s roughly a seventh of the week earning the low rate; annual seasonal closures matter more. A serious proposal shows the closed-day line instead of hoping you won’t ask. Same honesty applies to winter: November–January output in central Punjab falls well below summer (smog included), so insist on a monthly generation model, not a single annual average. And if you run evening or night shifts, that changes the self-use share — sometimes toward a battery, sometimes simply toward a smaller array.

## The caps and paperwork you must clear first

The 2026 Prosumer Regulations put hard edges around commercial systems:

- **Sanctioned load is the ceiling.** Your system may not exceed the sanctioned load on your connection. Many factories have headroom; verify before designing, because upgrading sanctioned load is its own DISCO process.
- **1 MW cap** on a prosumer facility.
- **250 kW and above** requires load-flow/technical studies before approval.
- **The 80% transformer rule** — new connections pause on transformers already 80%-full of solar — can bite in solar-dense industrial clusters. Worth checking early.
- **Fees:** a Rs 1,000-per-kW NEPRA processing fee plus the bidirectional meter, with approval realistically taking 4–14 weeks depending on the DISCO’s workload, as of mid-2026.

## Financing: capex is optional

As of mid-2026 — rates move, so treat these as the shape of the market rather than an offer:

### Bank financing

Solar lending is widely available at KIBOR-linked markups — landing around **15–22% a year** in mid-2026. Active products include Meezan Bank (Islamic, Diminishing Musharakah), Bank Alfalah’s Green Energy line (net-metered systems from 4 kW up), and JS Bank’s green-fund-backed programme with tenors up to 10 years for businesses. One caution: the old SBP concessional renewable-energy scheme ended in 2023 — anyone still advertising it is reading old brochures.

### Lease / Ijarah

Leasing companies and modarabas write asset-backed solar leases — conventional and Shariah-compliant — typically around five-year tenors, with a sweet spot of **50–500 kW**. Good fit when you want the asset without the upfront hit and prefer a fixed monthly line.

### PPA / BOOT — zero upfront

For roofs of **500 kW and above**, developer-financed structures are now standard in Pakistan: the developer builds, owns and operates the plant on your roof, you buy its output at a fixed per-unit tariff below your grid rate, and under BOOT terms the asset transfers to you at the end. No capex, savings from the first month — in exchange for a smaller slice of the total 20-year value than owning outright.

### The financed-payback caveat

Financing stretches payback, because the lender eats part of the saving. The deals still clear — grid power at Rs 45–60 is that expensive — but always ask for the **with-financing cashflow**, not just the cash-purchase one.

## What a bankable proposal includes

If you get one page with a big green arrow, you have marketing, not engineering. A proposal your bank (and your own accountant) will respect includes:

- Twelve months of consumption analysis from actual bills — not one flattering summer month
- Sanctioned-load verification and the DISCO process plan with a current, honest timeline
- A **monthly** generation model (summer/winter/smog), not an annual average
- The self-use vs export split, stated and justified from your operating hours
- Tariff assumptions and an escalation scenario, printed
- Itemised capex — panels, inverters, structure, protections, netmetering costs, taxes as separate lines
- O&M costs and panel degradation across the horizon
- A single-line diagram and a roof structural check
- The financing comparison, if you’re financing
