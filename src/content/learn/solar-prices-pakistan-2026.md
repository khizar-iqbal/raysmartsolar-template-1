The straight answer, as of July 2026: a properly installed 5 kW on-grid system with documented Tier-1 panels costs about **Rs 5.5–9 lakh** in the Lahore market, a 10 kW runs **Rs 9.5–15 lakh**, and lithium backup adds roughly **Rs 3–5 lakh per 5 kWh**. If a quote sits far below these bands, something inside it is not what it claims to be. Here is how to read the market — and any quote — properly.

## Why two “5 kW” quotes can differ by double

Ask three companies to price “a 5 kW system” and you may hear Rs 4.5 lakh, Rs 7 lakh and Rs 9 lakh — for what sounds like the same thing. It never is. Four differences do most of the damage:

### 1. The component tier

<span lang="ur-Latn">“Tier-1 panel aur branded inverter”</span> is what every salesman says. The Rs 4.5 lakh version often means B-grade, refurbished or relabelled panels wearing optimistic wattage stickers, plus an inverter nobody will service in year three. The gap between genuine hardware and “genuine” hardware is roughly the gap between the quotes.

### 2. Documented vs undocumented panels

A Pakistan-specific trap. *Documented* panels came through official import channels: real customs paperwork, serial numbers that verify in the manufacturer’s own app, and warranties a local office will actually honour. *Undocumented* panels may even be genuine — but with no paper trail, your 25-year warranty is a rumour. Documented costs more. It is the only kind worth buying.

### 3. The structure on your roof

A flush mount on a clean, unshaded roof is the cheap case. Elevated frames — to clear water tanks and shade lines, or to keep the roof usable underneath — add real steel and real money. A company that priced your roof without seeing it has guessed, and guessed low.

### 4. What’s bundled — and what’s quietly missing

Net-metering paperwork, government fees and the bidirectional meter can be inside the price, or waiting for you afterwards as a surprise. Market rates for the full net-metering package (documentation, meter, liaison) ran roughly Rs 80,000–220,000 in 2026 depending on system size and DISCO. Same story with batteries: a “hybrid-ready” price that doesn’t include the battery is an on-grid price in a costume.

## Panel prices right now

Wholesale Tier-1 panel rates in the Lahore market sat around **Rs 40–48 per watt** in early July 2026 — Jinko, LONGi, JA Solar and Canadian Solar N-type modules in the 585–645 W class. Two useful facts travel with that number:

- **It moves.** Panels jumped by several thousand rupees each in May–June 2026 while the market braced for a budget tax that, in the end, was not imposed. Rates have been settling since.
- **It sets a floor.** At Rs 40–48/W, the panels alone for a 5 kW system cost roughly Rs 2–2.4 lakh at wholesale. A complete installed 5 kW quote at Rs 4.5 lakh has spent half of itself on panels — the remainder must cover a real inverter, steel, copper, protections and labour. It can’t. Something gives, and it’s usually something you can’t see from the ground.

## What a battery adds

Lithium (LiFePO4) storage priced out at roughly **Rs 40,000–55,000 per kWh** for reputable brands — Pylontech, Dyness, GoodWe, Knox, BYD — as of mid-2026. As a rule of thumb, going from on-grid to hybrid with one ~5 kWh battery adds about **Rs 3–5 lakh** to the same-size system, counting the hybrid inverter premium. Whether that money is worth it depends on your outages and your evenings — we’ve compared [on-grid, hybrid and off-grid](/learn/on-grid-hybrid-off-grid) separately.

## Where taxes stand

As of July 2026: imported panels carry **10% GST**, in place since July 2025. The June 2026 budget floated raising it to 18%; after industry pushback — the Pakistan Solar Association’s “Stop the Sun Tax” campaign, as reported by pv-magazine — the increase was dropped from the final Finance Act. Duty changes on batteries and inverters were also discussed, and the final schedule there is less clear. The practical takeaway doesn’t change: solar taxes get revisited every June, and a fair quote shows the tax line separately, so you can see what is equipment and what is government levy.

## What a fair quote itemises

If it isn’t written down, it doesn’t exist. A quote worth signing names:

- **Panels** — brand, exact model, wattage and quantity, documented status, serials verifiable before final payment
- **Inverter** — brand and exact model, confirmed on your DISCO’s approved list for net metering
- **Structure** — flush or elevated, material and coating
- **Balance of system** — DC/AC cable brands and sizes, breakers, surge protection, earthing
- **Net metering** — government charges itemised at cost (currently a Rs 1,000-per-kW NEPRA processing fee plus the meter), not “free”, not missing
- **Taxes** — shown as their own line
- **Warranties** — what each covers and who honours it, in writing
- **Payment schedule** — staged against delivered work, never a lump sum up front

## Red flags in a cheap quote

- A final price given over the phone, before anyone has seen your roof
- “Tier-1” with no brand or model named — or a brand named but serial verification dodged
- No line for earthing, surge protection or cable specifications (this is where fires come from)
- “Free net metering” — the government fees are real; someone is either absorbing them (rare) or planning surprises (common)
- Verbal warranties — <span lang="ur-Latn">“25 saal ki guarantee, sir”</span> — with nothing on paper
- Pressure to pay a large advance today because “panel prices rise tomorrow”
